# Blood Relations — Reasoning Primer

## What this topic tests

Blood-relation problems give you a chain of statements like *"A is the brother of B; B is the daughter of C; C is married to D; D is the only son of E"* and ask a single question — often *"How is A related to E?"* The challenge is that the chain is given in narrative form and you have to translate it into a clean diagram or notation, then read off the answer.

The single most common reason students get these wrong is **doing the chain in their head instead of on paper**. With more than two links, working memory drops the gender or the direction. Always notate.

## The standard notation

Use these one-letter codes for relationships:

| Code | Means |
|------|-------|
| F | Father |
| M | Mother |
| H | Husband |
| W | Wife |
| S | Son |
| D | Daughter |
| B | Brother |
| Si | Sister |
| GF | Grandfather (paternal/maternal disambiguated by the chain) |
| GM | Grandmother |
| FB | Father's brother (uncle, paternal) |
| MB | Mother's brother (uncle, maternal) |

Compose them with `+` for "of": `F + B = FB` ("father's brother") = paternal uncle. `M + Si = MSi` = maternal aunt. The rule is to read RIGHT to LEFT: `F + B` means "B's F", which is "B's father" — but it's used as "father of B" in the chain. Pick one direction and stick to it.

A safer alternative: draw a small family tree.

## Drawing the family tree

Use these conventions:

- **Horizontal line** between two people = married
- **Vertical line** down from a couple = their children
- **`+`** suffix on a name = male, **`−`** suffix = female
- Place older generation higher

Example: *"X is married to Y; they have a son P and a daughter Q"*

```
   X+ ─── Y−
       |
   ┌───┴───┐
   P+      Q−
```

Now any subsequent statement adds nodes to this tree. Adding *"R is the brother of P"* gives R+ as a sibling of P — they share the same parents X and Y.

## Worked example

> *"A's mother is the only daughter of B. B is the father of C. How is C related to A?"*

Step 1: A's mother — call her M_A — is the *only* daughter of B. So B is M_A's father, and M_A has no sisters.

Step 2: B is the father of C. So C is a child of B. The chain doesn't say C's gender; treat it as unspecified.

Step 3: M_A and C share a parent (B). Are they the same person? No — M_A is the *only daughter* of B, but C might be male. So C is a sibling of M_A.

Step 4: M_A is A's mother. So C is the sibling of A's mother. **C is A's maternal uncle or aunt.**

If the question gave you C's gender (e.g., "C is male"), you could narrow to "maternal uncle".

## Common slip patterns and how to catch them

**Slip 1: Inverting the relation.** *"X is the daughter of Y"* means Y is X's parent, NOT X is Y's parent. Always check direction by reading the sentence twice.

**Slip 2: Assuming gender.** *"P is the child of Q"* doesn't fix P's gender. *"P is the only child of Q"* doesn't either. *"P is the only daughter"* fixes female; *"P is the only son"* fixes male.

**Slip 3: Skipping the "only" word.** *"R is the only son of S"* = R is male AND R has no brothers. The "only" carries information beyond the basic relation.

**Slip 4: Treating in-laws as blood relations.** *"A is the wife of B"* doesn't make A a blood relative of B's parents. The husband-wife edge is conjugal, not blood.

## How exam questions usually frame it

Three styles dominate:

1. **Direct chain.** Two-three sentences forming a path; ask the relationship between two endpoint people.
2. **Pointer at a photograph.** *"Pointing to a photograph, X says: 'He is the son of the only sister of my mother.'"* — translate the self-reference (*my*) and resolve.
3. **Coded relations.** Use symbols (P @ Q means P is the father of Q, etc.). Translate symbol-by-symbol then proceed.

For pointer-style questions, always anchor the speaker first ("my mother" = the speaker's mother), then trace.

## Final check

After you reach an answer, **verify by reversing the chain**. If C is A's maternal uncle, then A is C's nephew (or niece, depending on A's gender). Both directions should fit the original statements.

# रक्त संबंध — Reasoning Primer (हिन्दी)

## यह topic क्या test करता है

Blood-relation problems में chain दिया जाता है — *"A, B का भाई है; B, C की बेटी है; C, D से married है; D, E का इकलौता बेटा है"* — और एक सवाल पूछा जाता है, अक्सर *"A, E से कैसे related है?"* चुनौती यह है कि chain narrative में दी जाती है, और आपको उसे clean diagram या notation में translate करना है, फिर answer पढ़ना है।

सबसे बड़ी गलती **chain को mind में चलाना है, paper पर नहीं**। दो से ज़्यादा links हों तो working memory gender या direction drop कर देती है। हमेशा notate करें।

## Standard notation

Single-letter codes:

| Code | अर्थ |
|------|------|
| F | पिता |
| M | माता |
| H | पति |
| W | पत्नी |
| S | बेटा |
| D | बेटी |
| B | भाई |
| Si | बहन |
| FB | पिता का भाई (paternal uncle) |
| MB | माता का भाई (maternal uncle) |

Compose करने के लिए `+` का उपयोग: `F + B = FB` ("B का F" = B के पिता)। एक direction तय करें और उसी पर रहें।

## Family tree drawing

- **Horizontal line** = married
- **Vertical line down** = उनके children
- **`+` suffix** = male, **`−` suffix** = female
- पुरानी पीढ़ी ऊपर

## Worked example

> *"A की माता B की इकलौती बेटी है। B, C के पिता हैं। C का A से क्या रिश्ता है?"*

Step 1: A की माता (M_A) B की *इकलौती* बेटी है ⇒ B, M_A के पिता; M_A की कोई बहन नहीं।

Step 2: B, C के पिता ⇒ C, B का बच्चा। Gender unspecified।

Step 3: M_A और C एक ही parent (B) share करते हैं। M_A *इकलौती* बेटी है — C male हो सकता है। तो C, M_A का sibling।

Step 4: M_A, A की माता ⇒ **C, A का maternal uncle (या aunt)**।

## Common slips

- **दिशा उलटना**: *"X, Y की बेटी है"* का मतलब Y, X का parent है।
- **Gender मानना**: *"P, Q का बच्चा है"* P का gender fix नहीं करता।
- **"only" शब्द छोड़ना**: *"R, S का इकलौता बेटा है"* = R male AND R की कोई brother नहीं।
- **In-laws को blood रिश्ता समझना**: *"A, B की पत्नी है"* से A, B के parents की blood relative नहीं।

## Final check

Answer reach करने के बाद **chain को reverse करके verify करें**। अगर C, A का maternal uncle है, तो A, C का nephew/niece होगा। दोनों directions premises से fit होनी चाहिए।
